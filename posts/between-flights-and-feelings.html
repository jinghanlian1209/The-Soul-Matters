Big Big World

Between Flights and Feelings

Everyone loves Paris.

April 1, 2026Somewhere over Europe6 min read

Last night, I was drinking at a friend’s place. When it was over, Mei and I walked toward the bus stop, and she suddenly said to me, “I’m so glad I met you. You really are such a wonderful person.”

I turned to look at her, and beneath the overwhelming tenderness in her eyes, my heart gave a sudden, quiet leap.

Today, I walked home from class with my backpack on, under another impossibly beautiful afternoon sun. The sun is always this beautiful here. Before class had even ended, I was already restless, making plans for happy hour in the group chat.

They said they wanted to hear my stories.

I laughed and asked, “How do you know I have another interesting story to tell?”

They said, “Because we all know you’re brave. Most qualities can be imitated, but courage is rare. And naturally, a brave person will live a life full of stories worth telling.”

I could hardly believe Americans were saying something like this to me.

It suddenly occurred to me that they understood me, and cared about me, far more deeply than I had imagined.

A few days ago, when I returned to work after the break, I realized that all my coworkers knew who I was. They knew I was the funny little girl in the office, a twenty-three-year-old graduate student with the face of an eighteen-year-old. One after another, they asked whether I had lost my luggage again during my trip to Europe.

They even remembered me as Jinghan, not Julia.

And I was the one who could barely remember their names.

I do love Paris, but that love is wrapped in too many filters. Perhaps it is because I have always known Paris as a visitor. My love for it is more spiritual than practical, the way one loves painting or poetry.

But no one can spend a lifetime contemplating the so-called meaning of life. No one can remain forever inside their own romanticism. In a place like that, both happiness and pain become magnified without limit, until one begins to fall into emptiness.

A person cannot spend an entire life only painting and writing poems.

Los Angeles is different.

Perhaps what I feel for LA is more physical, more instinctive. It is the kind of love one has for eating and sleeping. There is no need to think too much. Going to work and school beneath the sun already feels meaningful enough.

There is hardly time to sink into melancholy. Each morning, the sun rises again, and everything becomes full of hope.

I told my friend that the moment I return to LA, I feel my feet touch the ground again.

I can stand beneath the sunlight with a bare face, wearing a comfortable tank top and low-rise American jeans, and even breathing feels freer. I can return to the most unpolished version of myself.

LA will make room for me.

I am allowed to be awkward. I am allowed to shine with sweat beneath the sun. I am allowed to know nothing about fashion.

Europe is a place that makes emotions come easily. It is as though the senses become more delicate there, and every passing thought, every tiny moment, expands beyond its original shape.

I would play *Mystery of Love* over and over in my headphones, sitting beside the Seine, feeling my eyes slowly fill with tears as I watched the sunset appear and disappear.

No wonder I love it.

But also, no wonder I cannot stay for too long.

In the romantic wind, beside that sentimental river, I would sink too deeply into myself. I would think endlessly about romanticism and idealism. I would wish for time to stop inside certain moments of unbearable beauty, and then I would no longer want to move forward.

Sunlight feels more beautiful when it is scarce.

Spring feels more precious because it is always leaving.

And so I want to hold on with all my strength. I want to stay.

Europe is the undertone of my personality. It is the dampness left behind after the tide rises at night. It is uncertainty, separation anxiety, and a quiet pessimism toward love and human connection.

I am always trying desperately to hold on to something, as though it will disappear the moment I loosen my grip.

The spring light in Europe, for example.

LA sunlight is nothing like that.

It feels eternal.

It leaves less room for overthinking. It pushes you forward. There is no time to linger too long inside sadness.

Here, there is always a fast song playing, something with a strong beat.

And before you know it, there is no time left to be sad.

A line I want to keep

“Perhaps belonging is not a place, but the courage to keep arriving.”

Made with too many thoughts and just enough courage.
