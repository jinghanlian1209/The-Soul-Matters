Someone Was Glad

My friend wrote me a 7-page letter, I'll keep it forever.

May 29, 2026Cambridge 10 min read
✈ delayed♫ Mystery of Love♡ touching

You’re gone now, and the quiet you left is the reason I’m finally writing this down. I forget things — I’ve had to learn how — and I don’t want to forget what your being means to me. So let me try to tell you the whole of it.

I should start with something I’ve really not said or admit somewhat. I’ve been alone a long time — not for any lack of people, there were always people, but in the sense that no one would quite follow me into the place where I actually live. Since we were kids, the things that moved me most — physics, certain kinds of beauty, the plain strange way the world is put together — were things I had no one to hand to, so I felt all of it by myself. I couldn’t stay that hungry forever; i believe no one can. So I did the reasonable thing and made the solitude into a home. I told myself I didn’t need a witness, that the things that matter most happen in private anyway, and most days I believed it. What I really did was teach myself not to feel the thirst — which isn’t the same as no longer needing the water. And somewhere in there, if I’m honest with you, the nature of solitude stopped being only strength and turned into a place in shade.

Then you came. Not a colleague, not someone impressed by what I can do — you, who knew me when I was a child, who is fond of me and not of my mind, who crossed the airport at a run to reach me after eight years of almost nothing. You were glad to see me. I don’t think you know what that did. After years of being valued, at best, for my intelligence and otherwise left to myself by the human side of this world, to have someone be plainly glad I exist hands me back my own personhood — for a moment I am a person again, and not a capability.

The truest thing is the hardest to say straight, so let me come at it sideways. The most alive I ever am is when I’m alone in the natural world; it doesn’t only give me something beautiful to look at, it teaches me how to feel in the first place. That has always been a solitary thing, and it had to be, because there was never anyone in that room with me. So when we walked across Boston for a whole night, and came out at the sea just as it began to lighten — that wasn’t company, exactly. It was that the most private room I have, the one I’d quietly accepted I would always be alone in, had you standing inside it. I feel a great deal, and I’ve always felt it by myself; for a few days I didn’t have to. And this part matters to me, so let me be clear: you didn’t make me want to stop being alone. You walked into a room I already live in and love, and you turned on a floor lamp. The change isn’t that I’ll no longer be alone. It’s that I can be alone and still be reached — that being by myself and being out of reach were never the same thing. You didn’t take my solitude from me. You warmed it.

Here’s the part that surprised me, because this hasn’t been a kind year. I’m low right now — doubting myself, feeling slow and stupid, taking my own work apart and starting it over.While you’d shine. It lifted me, and I had to stop and work out why.

This is what I landed on: I’m not moved by arguments, I’m moved by people — and you are a hard person to argue with. You told me about your confusions, the things you don’t have answers to yet, the things you’ve been through. And you smile a lot. It’s a child’s smile with nothing held back, nothing folded underneath it, just plainly, simply happy, the way little kids are before they learn not to be. You came through every hard thing your own and it hasn’t clouded you. You name what confuses and troubles you, out loud, and then grin like a kid, and somehow that is braver than any amount of armor.

So I can’t reason myself into believing the world makes room for stubborn, idealistic lives. But I can watch you, whom I’ve set to since we were children — live exactly that, and then it stops being a claim I have to defend and becomes a fact with a face. it is so alive.

That’s how the dust came off, too. Not probably from what you said — we walked eighteen hours and exchanged a couple. It was just that vitality is warm, and I’d been cold a long time, and I’d half-forgotten the fire had ever been mine. The part of me that throws itself all the way into whatever it loves hadn’t died; it had only gone tired and dim. That kind of fire doesn’t come back through effort. It comes back from being near someone who’s burning — and you were burning, and a few days near it were enough.

Two quieter things have stayed with me. The first: I’d assumed I had simply disappeared from everyone I left behind — that the long silence meant the connection was dead, and, worse, that I was the one who let it die. Then you told me you’d turn to me at those important turn in your life, that things I once said had helped you. So I wasn’t as absent as I believed. Something of me had been out in the world the whole time, doing a little good in your life, through years I thought I’d spent alone even in hindsight. That’s an enormous, strange relief — with an ache folded into it, because I never knew.

The second: you’ve kept a version of me that I’d buried under everything I became. You looked at me across eight years you never saw — none of the long middle, none of how I got here — and knew me at once. The boy I used to be isn’t lost. He’s safe with you. Seeing him again reminded me how far I’ve come, down a road I’d kept my head down on for so long that I’d forgotten it was a road.

And there’s a grief running under all of it, which is, in the end, why I’m writing it down. You came, and then you left, and I’m sitting here trying to hold onto something already gone — to get it onto the page before it dims, before I forget it the way I’ve trained myself, out of necessity, to forget.

So let me just say the rest of it plainly, the part I couldn’t get out at the airport with you standing right there. I’m sorry I went quiet for so long; I didn’t know how not to. Having you here, being known by someone who knew me before any of this, helped me more than I could say to your face, and moved me more than I thought I could still be moved. I am completely happy for you, and there’s nothing complicated in it. Whatever you came through to still be able to smile like that — I saw it, and it moved something in me that hasn’t settled since. Your life is going to be a brilliant one; watching you these few days, I think it already is. Don’t let it be eight years again.

And the smallest thing of all, which turned out to be all that: thank you for being glad to see me.

A line I want to keep

“thank you for being glad to see me.”

Made with too many thoughts and just enough courage.
